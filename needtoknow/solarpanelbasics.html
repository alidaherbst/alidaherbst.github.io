Solar Panel Basics




A solar panel is a panel designed to absorb the sun's rays as a source of energy for generating electricity or heating.
They are called "solar" panels because most of the time, the most powerful source of light available is the Sun, called Sol by astronomers.
Some scientists call them photovoltaics which means, basically, "light-electricity."

Facts about Solar Panels.

  • The glass substrate of a solar panel allows light to shine upon the silicon surface and produce electricity from the movement of electrons on the atomic level.
  • Solar panels work in both a direct and a diffuse light manner.
  • Silicon is a very good conductor but doesn’t have the ability to produce electrons when light strikes its surface. In order to generate electricity, a flow of electrons and impurities are introduced into the silicon used.
  • The electrical current generated by the light flows from the solar panel into batteries for storage in the form of DC electricity.
  • In order for the elecricity produced by the solar panels to be used in place of fossit fuel electricity, it has to be turned into AC electricity. This is accomplished by running the stored electricity through an inverter that makes the DC into AC.
  • The electricity can be used to power your house. Any extra electricity produced can be sold back to the utility company that you get your electricity from to offset your bill or if, you generate more than you use, then you can realize a profit.
  • Solar panels were originally only used in space. Solar panels now provide energy for places that are remote and that cannot use electrical lines.
  • The price of solar panels has dropped in the recent years due to the greater ability of manufacturers to create pure silicon wafers that are the starting material of a solar panel, making the use of solar panels more widespread.

Terminology.

Lets discuss some basic terminology often used when discussing solar panels or photovoltaics (PV) panels.

Photovoltaic cell.



A photovoltaic cell, also called a "solar cell" are the basic building blocks of solar panels.
A solar cell, or photovoltaic cell, is an electrical device that converts the energy of light directly into electricity by the photovoltaic effect, which is a physical and chemical phenomenon.
It is a form of photoelectric cell, defined as a device whose electrical characteristics, such as current, voltage, or resistance, vary when exposed to light.
The common type is a wafer made of pure silicon, one of the most abundant materials on earth.
This wafer is "doped" with minute quantities of boron, phosphorous, gallium or other exotic sounding materials.
Each cell develops about 0.5 volts of DC electrical potential when exposed to light.
The maximum current it can produce depends on the cell's surface area and the intensity of the light.
PV cells can produce electricity for up to 50 years! The energy that was required to manufacture them will be generated by the PV cell in only a few years.
The operation of a photovoltaic (PV) cell requires 3 basic attributes:

  • The absorption of light, generating either electron-hole pairs or excitons.
  • The separation of charge carriers of opposite types.
  • The separate extraction of those carriers to an external circuit.

Module or solar panel.




Mulltiple solar cells in an integrated group, all oriented in one plane, constitute a solar photovoltaic panel or solar photovoltaic module.
Solar cells are usually connected in series in modules, creating an additive voltage.
Connecting cells in parallel yields a higher current; however, problems such as shadow effects can shut down the weaker (less illuminated) parallel string (a number of series connected cells) causing substantial power loss and possible damage.
Traditionally modules with 36 cells were made, producing around 18 volts for a 12 volt nominal output (36 x 0.5 V = 18 V).
Since the advent of MPPT (maximum power point trackers) technology, modules with more cells are being made, producing higher voltages.
Note: Only modules with 36 cells (with Vmp around 18 volts) are suitable for use with PWM type controllers.

Array.




This term describes the whole group of modules that make up a system. These can be many series strings in parallel, depending on the design.
Such an array could be mounted on multiple racks and sometimes the term "sub array" is used to mean one rack of modules in a multiple-rack array.

Series string.


When we connect electrical sources in series, the current remains the same but the voltage is increased, which is exactly the goal of a string!
Solar cells are usually connected in series in modules, creating an additive voltage.
It is the same as when you put three 1.5 Volt cells in your torch - they are in series to build the voltage to 3 x 1.5 = 4.5 V.

Larger off grid PV systems can operate at voltages of up to 150 VDC and grid-tied systems can use up to 600 volts.
A series string can also be paralleled with other series strings which will increase the charging current.

Parallel string.


When we connect electrical sources in parallel, the voltage remains the same but the current increaseds by the number of Amps!
The concept of series and parallel is similar when connecting batteries

Power Expectations.
Solar panels very rarely produces their rated power!

FACTOR TYPICAL DERATING
Temperature 0.88
Power tolerance 0.95
Diodes and connections 0.995
Module mismatch 0.98
DC wire losses 0.97
Soiling (dirt and dust) 0.95
TOTAL 0.75

The biggest loss occurs from the realistic cell temperature this is typically about 12%
As seen in the table above other factors causing the derating of power are the loerance, DC wire losses and even soil or dirt.
Realistically one can expect up to 75% loss of its rated power!!.

Panel Specs.
When buying solar panels study their specs in order to make sure you have the right panels for your requirement.

Temperature.

Usually the Standard test conditions (STC) given for a panel is 25°. Realistically we know that is very seldom the case.
The power temperature correction factor shoudl be given on a spec sheet. In this example power will reduce by 0.4% for every degree above 25°(STC)!
Realistic cell temperatures can easily be 55 degrees plus which in this example would reduce a 250Wp to 220Watts.
55°C -25°C = 30° x (0.4% of 250) = 30 x 1 Thus 250 – 30 = 220 Watts.
On a lovely hot sunny day a 250 Wp module will effectively produce 220 Watts of power!

Power Tolerance.

In the example above the power tolerance is given as 0- +5 Watt for the 250Wp. Therefore minimum 250 Watts and Masimum 255 Watts.
But wait there is small print!!



According to the small print the 250 Minimum Watts could be onlty 237.5 Watts at STC conditions!
250 – 5% = 237.5 Wp!
Thus if we take the 237.5 watts and include the temperature factor we end up with effective watts of 209 watts!
237.5 – (30° x (0.4% of 237.5) = 209 watts

Further losses in effective watts can occur with wires, module mismatch, soil as well as diodes and connections!

It is extremely important to understand the warranty on your solar panel.

A linear warranty is better than the standard tiered warranty!
